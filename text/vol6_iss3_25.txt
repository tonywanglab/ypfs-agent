Russia: Otkritie Bank Restructuring, 20171 
Benjamin Hoffner2 
Yale Program on Financial Stability Case Study

Abstract 
In July and August 2017, Otkritie Bank, Russia’s largest privately  owned bank, experienced 
a deposit run related to concerns over Otkritie’s recent acquisitions. The run prompted 
Otkritie’s shareholders to approach the Central Bank of Russia (CBR) for assistance . On  
August 29, 2017 , the CBR announced a rescue plan for Otkritie. In it , the CBR p ledged to 
become Otkritie’s main investor using a newly created resolution mechanism wherein the 
CBR would take at least a 75% equity stake using funds from the Fund for Banking Sector 
Consolidation, a subdivision of the CBR . The CBR simultaneously appoint ed a provisional 
administration, composed of CBR employees , to perform an audit, and in the process 
discovered a capital hole amounting to 188.9 billion Russian rubles (RUB; USD 3.3 billion) as 
of October 1, 2017 . As a result, t he CBR moved to  bail-in about RUB 160 billion in 
subordinated debt and writing equity down to RUB 1, after the Duma passed amendments 
to the bankruptcy law authorizing the CBR-appointed provisional administration  to do so . 
The CBR then purchased RUB 456.2 billion (99.9% equity) in newly issued ordinary shares 
to cover Otkritie’s capital deficit and extend financial assistance to Otkritie’s related entities. 
In March 2018, the CBR restructure d Otkritie via a merger with a smaller bailed -out bank, 
B&N. At the same time, the CBR creat ed a bad bank to take over a portion of Otkritie’s 
noncore assets. In June 2018, the CBR injected an additional RUB 42.7 billion in new capital 
in Otkritie, mostly to provide relief to related entities. In December 2022, the CBR sold 
Otkritie to state -owned (and former shareholder) VTB Bank for RUB 340 billion , as 
compared to the RUB 499 billion of capital support. 
Keywords: ad hoc capital injection,  bankruptcy prevention, CBR, Fund for Banking Sector 
Consolidation, Otkritie, resolution, restructuring

Overview 
This case study  is about the resolution and 
restructuring of Otkritie Bank. For details of 
the recapitalization of the bank, see Hoffner 
(2024); details of the emergency liquidity 
assistance provided to Otkritie can be found 
in Hoffner (forthcoming). 
Otkritie Bank was established in 1992 and 
entered retail banking in 2012. It grew  from 
relative obscurity to become Russia’s largest 
privately owned bank by 2015 (CBR 2017d; 
Fitzgeorge-Parker 2017; Seddon 2017a) . In 
its rise, Otkritie capitalized on opportunities 
to absorb smaller, distressed lenders, 
partially financed by cheap state funding 
under Russia’s unconventional resolution 
framework. This framework, which was in 
place between 2006 and 2017, required, as 
the only alternative to bankruptcy, a private 
investor to take over a distressed bank. In 
return, the Central Bank of Russia (CBR) or 
the Deposit Insurance Agency (DIA) —with 
funding from CBR —extended cheap long -
term loans to the investor or the distressed 
bank (Fitzgeorge-Parker 2017; Karpova and 
Kruglikova 2018).  
Several problems emerged from this 
rehabilitation program.  Lacking official 
oversight, some investors left the 
mismanagement of acquired banks in place, 
used their balance sheets to offload toxic 
assets, and deployed the proceeds from the 
rehabilitation loans for purposes other than recapitalizing the distressed banks (Fitzgeorge-
Parker 2017; Invest Profit 2018). The program also effectively exempted a large share of the 
banking sector from basic regulatory requirements because supervisors eased standards on 
acquired banks . Otkritie Bank was among four private banks, co llectively referred to as 
“Garden Ring banks,” that frequently acquired distressed banks with government help 
through the program and in the process expanded their balance sheets by several times 
between 2012 and 2016 (Fitzgeorge-Parker 2017). 
In 2016, rec ognizing the flaws in the resolution scheme, CBR Governor Elvira Nabiullina 
called for a reform of Russia’s bankruptcy laws. Those reforms, which the Duma passed in 
May and became effective in June 2017, enabled the CBR or DIA to invest directly  in the

eve of the CBR’s August 29 announcement that it would rescue Otkritie (Kulikova 2017). CBR 
Deputy Chair Dmitry Tulin believed that Otkritie’s acquisition of Rosgosstrakh was the 
ultimate reason for Otkritie’s collapse. When the CBR announced the rescue, both Trust Bank 
and Rosgosstrakh were included in the perimeter of Otkritie’s rehabilitation (Meylanova 
2017). A Russian news article in September suggested that these tra nsactions likely served 
to offload toxic assets onto Otkritie’s balance sheet at the expense of the CBR’s eventual 
bailout through capital injections (Kulikova 2017). 
On August 29, 2017, following a request from Otkritie’s shareholders, the CBR announced 
the bailout of Otkritie using —for the first time —the newly created resolution mechanism, 
the Fund for Banking Sector Consolidation (FBSC) (Interfax 2017a; Polyakova, Samuseva, 
and Tatyana 2017) . While guaranteeing the continuity of Otkritie’s operations, th e CBR 
replaced management with a provisional administration, mainly composed of CBR 
employees, to run the bank and conduct an audit of the bank’s financial condition.  
Following the audit, the CBR planned to inject capital, leaving the CBR with at least a  75% 
stake in Otkritie, as required by law (Russian Federation 2002d, art. 189.49). The 75% equity 
stake would increase depending on the capital shortfall (CBR 2017d; Dementyeva et al. 2017; 
WPS USA 2017b) . Though initially stating that creditors would not be bailed in, the CBR 
decided to bail in subordinated debt holders as a prerequisite for recapitalization after the 
audit revealed a capital hole  that amounted to  RUB 188.9 billion by October 1, 2017  (CBR 
2017d; Fojcik 2017a; Kalyukov, Titova, and Pashutin skaya 2017; WPS USA 2017b) . By 
December 2017, following amendments to the Bankruptcy Law, Otkritie’s provisional 
administration had written off subordinated debts worth RUB 160 billion and reduced the 
authorized capital to RUB 1 (Fojcik 2017b).

On Decembe r 11, the CBR announced that it had recapitalized Otkritie Bank with RUB 
456.2 billion through the purchase of new ordinary shares, amounting to a 99.9% stake (CBR 
2017h; CBR 2017i). Of the RUB 456.2 billion, RUB 189.1 billion covered the capital deficit, 
RUB 182 billion provided additional capital to the bank, and the remainder was allocated for 
financial assistance to troubled Otkritie subsidiaries, including Rosgosstrakh insurance 
company and nonstate pension funds (CBR 2017h) . The CBR injected a further  
RUB 42.7 billion into Otkritie in June 2018, to be used to extend financial support to various 
subsidiaries to compensate for the write-downs on claims between the entities (CBR 2018b). 
Following Otkritie’s bailout, the CBR also intervened in bailing out several other banks: B&N 
in September 2017 , Promsvyazbank in December 2017 , Asia-Pacific Bank in April 2018 , 
Moscow Industrial Bank in January 2019, and Vokbank in April 2019 (Interfax 2019). These 
bailouts began with liquidity support to guarantee the continuity of operations, followed by 
recapitalizations in which, following the bankruptcy law, the government took a stake of 
75% or more, effectively putting the banks into receivership or nationalization (CBR 2017f; 
CBR 2018c).  
In March 2018, the CBR announced the merger of B&N Bank with Otkritie, which was 
finalized on January 1, 2019, following the conversion of B&N’s shares into Otkritie shares 
(CBR 2018a; CBR 2019). The CBR also announced the creation of a noncore asset bank 
(NAB), through a merger of Trust Bank (a subsidiary of Otkritie) and Rost Bank (a subsidiary 
of B&N), both of which had been acquired by their respective parent companies under the 
old resolution framework ( that is , through long -term loans from the CBR  and DIA ) (CBR 
2019; Interfax 2018; Seddon 2018). The CBR provide d the bad bank with a RUB 1.1 trillion 
loan at a 0.5% interest rate. The bad bank recover ed and liquidated the distressed assets 
over time, returning to the CBR the funds allocated for resolution (Larina 2019; Seddon 
2018).  
In July 2019, the CBR completed its bankruptcy prevention measures for Otkritie, with the 
bank having met its rehabilitation targets. In 2019, Otkritie paid dividends of RUB 2 billion 
for 2018. Thereafter, the CBR developed a plan to divest its stake in Otkritie (CBR 2020).  
In December 2022, the CBR sold 100% of its stake in  Otkritie to state-owned VTB Bank for 
RUB 340 billion. The passage of a new law in March 2022 enabled the CBR to sell shares of 
Otkritie at the market value, as estimated by an independent appraiser, AO Business 
Solutions and Technologies, at RUB 328 billi on to RUB 374 billion. VTB paid RUB 233.1 
billion in cash and RUB 106.9 billion in federal government bonds (CBR 2022). Figure 1

Summary Evaluation 
One paper by Drobyshevsky, Trunin, and Khromov (2017), from a Russian economic think 
tank, finds that the CBR’s resolution of Otkritie and B&N Bank, via the FBSC, “can be regarded 
under the circumstances as [sic] optimal solu tion” given the CBR’s position as the 
preeminent banking regulator and its experience in other areas of bank resolution (11). The 
authors compare the alternative of the CBR ’s revoking the banks’ licenses as likely to have

caused a greater run, and having the DIA subsequently manage the bankruptcy process . At 
the time of the CBR’s intervention, the DIA lacked funding, and given the size of the banks in 
distress, new bank contributions to the DIA could not have covered the scale of potential 
reimbursements. As was precedent, the CBR could have financed the restructuring by 
lending to the DIA, which managed bankruptcy proceedings, though this m echanism was 
often costlier than just reimbursing insured deposits (Drobyshevsky, Trunin, and Khromov 
2017).  
One investigative report look s into the Russian bank resolutions particularly before the 
introduction of the new mechanism and finds evidence of corruption in the DIA’s handling 
of bankruptcy proceedings (Yakoreva, Safronov, and Badanin 2020) . The authors attribute 
the exorbitant costs and corruption associated with the DIA’s bankruptcy management as a 
motivating factor in the CBR’s creating and using the new resolution framework outside the 
DIA’s control. Russia’s Federal Antimonopoly Service criticized the DIA for its opaque hiring 
practice for contractors (Yakoreva, Safronov, and Badanin 2020).  
The International Monetary Fund (IMF) 2020 Article I V report provide d broad, positive 
feedback on the CBR’s new resolution mechanism, stating that the FBSC “has proven effective 
in closing banks that are assessed as fundamentally non -viable and in rescuing those that 
need to be rescued” (IMF 2021, 18). 
Co-founder of Otkritie Boris Mints has criticized the CBR’s handling of the intervention, 
arguing that the CBR should have publicly pledged to support Otkritie in early July 2017, 
rather than waiting until Otkritie had run out of liquidity in August (Pugsley 2021).

Key Design Decisions 
1. Purpose: The Central Bank of Russia rescued Otkritie Bank, Russia’s largest 
private bank, to prevent its bankruptcy , giv en its systemic importance , and to 
ensure its capital met regulatory requirements. 
In July 2017, Russian rating agency ACRA assigned a BBB - rating to  Otkritie Bank —a 
systemically important institution and Russia’s largest privately owned bank —citing 
concerns about Otkritie’s loan book from recent acquisitions (Novye Izvestiia 2017; Seddon 
2017b; Seddon 2017c). Over the subsequent two months, Otkritie also finalized a series of 
unprofitable acquisitions  of struggling banks and other financial institutions with 
government approval, following the previous resolution policy. Meanwhile, large deposit 
outflows resulted in Otkritie’s losing RUB 611 billion in deposits, or 20% of its balance sheet 
(Seddon 2017d).  On August 29, 2017, following a request from Otkritie’s shareholders, the 
CBR announced the rescue of Otkritie Bank using for the first time a newly created resolution 
mechanism, the Fund for Banking Sector Consolidation (CBR 2017d). Under this mechanism, 
the CBR would take at least a 75% equity stake after appointing a provisional administration 
to assess the bank’s financials (Mokeeva and Popova 2020). At the time of the intervention, 
Otkritie was the eighth-largest bank in Russia by assets, with RUB 2.5 trillion assets and RUB 
539 billion of citizens’ savings and deposits (CBR 2017d; Dementyeva et al. 2017; Karpova 
and Kruglikova 2018).  
While other private banks volunteered to participate in the bailout under the old resolution 
scheme (in other words, by receiving a loan from the CBR), the CBR opted for the new scheme 
by first appointing a temporary administration and ultimately nationalizing the bank 
following a RUB 456.2 billion recapitalization in December 2017 (Dementyeva et al. 2017; 
Interfax 2020). The CBR justified the usage of the new resolution mechanism on the basis of 
decreasing bailout expenditures, enhancing monitoring during resolution, and expediting 
the process of rehabilitation, three areas where the old resolution scheme fell short (CBR 
2018c; Invest Profit 2018). 
The recapitalization aimed to make Otkritie compliant with regulatory capital requirements, 
and in December 2017, the temporary administration’s tenure ended with the creation of 
new permanent management bodies (CBR 2017h; Otkritie 2018a) . However, by January 1, 
2018, Otkritie was noncompliant with several regulatory requirements , and on January 19, 
2018, the CBR announced that it would continue  operating the bank as a going concern 
(Otkritie 2018a). In March 2018, the CBR announced the merger of another bailed out bank, 
B&N, with Otkritie, which was finalized on January 1, 2019, following the conversion of 
B&N’s shares into Otkritie ’s. The CBR also announced the creation of a noncore asset bank, 
through a merger of Trust Bank (a former subsidiary of Otkritie, which the CBR nationalized 
in March ) and Rost Bank (subsidiary of B&N)  (CBR 2019; Interfax 2018; Otkritie 2018a; 
Seddon 2018).  
In July 2018, following the merger of Trust and Rost into the NAB (hereinafter Trust NAB), 
the CBR decided to restructure Otkritie by establishing a bridge bank for its noncore assets,

2. Part of a Package: The CBR provided significant liquidity relief to Otkritie through 
unsecured lending and a new emergency facility.  
Before the bailout of Otkritie, the CBR extended substantial liquidity support both through 
its standing facilities and in ad hoc forms. In August, Otkritie had borrowed RUB 728 billion 
from the CBR, which included an unsecured ad hoc loan (Seddon 2017f). The CBR  had not 
provided unsecured loans since the Global Financial Crisis of 2007–2009 (WPS USA 2017a). 
By January 1, 2018, Otkritie had outstanding RUB 380 billion in “short-term” liquidity 
support from the CBR (CBR 2018c). The CBR provided such liquidity support in the form of 
deposits using funds from the FBSC (CBR 2018d; CBR 2019). 
On September 1 (announced on September 4), the CBR introduced a new, broadly defined 
emergency liquidity facility for banks that had exhausted all other liquidity sources. 
Eligibility was at the discretion of the CBR, which would consider a bank’s systemic 
importance and solvency before approving draws . Approved banks could pledge collateral  
outside the CBR’s Lombard List (collateral accepted for standard facilities), claims on 
nonfinancial institutions, and other types of securities. Liquidity could take the form of a repo 
and/or secured loan, depending on the collateral, and would have a ma ximum term of 90 
days and a rate of 1.75% over the CBR policy rate (CBR 2017e). See the forthcoming YPFS 
case study on the CBR’s ad hoc emergency liquidity support to Otkritie  (Hoffner 
forthcoming). 
3. Legal Authority: The CBR resolved Otkritie under a new legal mechanism added 
to Russia’s bankruptcy law; the CBR waited for additional amendments to enable 
bail-in before recapitalizing Otkritie. 
The legal authority for the CBR to inject capital into a distressed bank is e numerated in the 
June 2017 amendments to the 2002 Federal Law on the Central Bank of the Russian 
Federation (CBR Law) and the 2002 Law on Insolvency (Bankruptcy Law) (Russian 
Federation 2002b; Russian Federation 2002c). The Duma amended both laws in the months 
before Otkritie’s rescue with the legal adoption of the CBR’s new rehabilitation (bankruptcy 
prevention) mechanism. Pursuant to Article 46 of the Central Bank Law, the CBR’s board of 
directors decides on the implementation of resolution measures (Russian Federation 2002a, 
art. 46). 
Under Russia’s Bankruptcy Law, a recapitalization from the CBR required it to take over at 
least 75% of the bank’s share capital (Russian Federation 2002d, art. 189.49).  
Article 189.10 of the Bankruptcy Law outlines the following conditions under which the CBR 
may resolve a bank (Kinash 2019; Russian Federation 2002d, art. 189.10):

The CBR La w and Bankruptcy Law prescribe the CBR to use , for the purposes of bank 
resolution, funds from the FBSC, a subdivision of the CBR (Russian Federation 2002a, arts. 
75–76). With regard to banks under resolution, s uch uses cover: acquiring bank shares 
(including in excess of their nominal value)  and selling such shares (including below the 
purchase price) , extending subordinated credits (deposits, loans, and bonded loans), and 
acquiring and alienating property rights and claims to said banks (Russian Federatio n 
2002a, arts. 66 –67). The CBR could also transfer claims on and property of banks under 
resolution to the FBSC Asset Management Company (FBSC AMC) for trust management and 
for the purposes of establishing a unit investment fund; the CBR could also extend financing 
to the FBSC AMC to participate in resolution measures (Russian Federation 2002a, arts. 66–
67). The FBSC AMC is a separate limited liability company, wholly owned by the CBR, acting 
as a special purpose vehicle in bank resolution (CBR 2018c; Russian Federation 2002a, arts. 
74–76). This vehicle served as the trustee for the CBR’s stake in Otkritie. 
The CBR is also entitled to appoint a temporary administration to a distress ed bank for a 
term of up to six months (Russian Federation 2002a, art. 63).  
On October 30,  2017, the CBR -appointed provisional administration failed to reach the 
necessary quorum (shareholders in attendance) at Otkritie’s shareholder meeting to bail  in 
creditors (Astapenko 2017; WPS USA 2017b) . The CBR requested amendments to t he 
Bankruptcy Law, which the Duma passed on November 25. On November 29, the CBR 
transferred the powers of provisional administration to the FBSC AMC, which enabled the 
provisional administration to bail  in the subordinated creditors and equity holders (CBR 
2017g; Prime News 2017b).

4. Administration: The CBR administered the resolution through the FBSC. 
Following a change to Russian  bankruptcy laws in May 2017, the CBR acted as the primary 
resolution authority. Before the change, resolution typically centered  on the DIA , which  
would extend cheap long -term loans, provided by the CBR, to willing private buyers of 
distressed banks. The old model was deemed inefficient, resulting in drawn -out and 
expensive resolution processes with little oversight (Mokeeva and Popova 2020). As a result, 
from 2017 onward, the CBR has administered resolution using its newly created CBR 
subdivision, the FBSC, with Otkritie being the first use case (Mokeeva and Popova 2020).  
Under this new mechanism, the CBR’s board of directors makes a decision to im plement 
resolution for a distressed bank (under the conditions specified in Key Design Decision No. 
3, Legal Authority) and subsequently appoints a temporary administration , which 
temporarily limits or revokes  the former management’s powers (Mokeeva and Po pova 
2020). In Otkritie’s case, the temporary administration initially consisted of CBR employees 
(CBR 2017a) before the CBR transferred the responsibilities of the temporary administration to 
the FBSC AMC in November 2017 (also largely CBR employees) (CBR 2017b; CBR 2017g).  
The temporary administration’s primary responsibilities are to restore the bank’s solvency, 
“create an efficient managerial mechanism, eliminate revealed violations, and take other 
measures to” improve the bank’s health (Mokeeva and Popova 2020, 13).  
After the temporary administration audits the bank’s financials and makes an assessment on 
the allocation of additional funds, the CBR takes over at least 75% of the bank’s share capital, 
followed by the appointment of permanent management bodies (Mokeeva and Popova 
2020). As noted, the CBR administers funds for restructuring through the FBSC, the assets of 
which are managed in trust by the FBSC AMC (Mokeeva and Popova 2020).  
5. Governance: The CBR’s board of directors made resolution-related decisions, and 
the CBR’s chief auditor assessed the operations of the resolution administrator, 
the FBSC. 
The CBR’s chief auditor assesse d the operations of the FBSC, which administered Otkritie’s 
resolution under direction from the CBR’s board of directors (CBR 2019; Russian Federation 
2002a, arts. 25, 46 ). The National Financial Board appointed the CBR’s chief auditor, who 
was directly accountable to the CBR chairman (Russian Federation 2002a, arts. 13, 76, 95). 
The National Financial Board was composed of 12 members, which, in addition to the CBR 
chairman, included: two delegates from the Federation Council, three from the State Duma, 
three from the Russian president, and three from the Russian governm ent (Russian 
Federation 2002a, art. 12). The chief auditor assessed the efficiency of the FBSC’s resolution 
operations along four criteria:

injection (CBR 2018c). 
6. Communication and Disclosure: The CBR explained the decision to rescue Otkritie 
in press releases and interviews on the basis of its systemic importance . The 
central bank initially stated that it would not impose a moratorium on payments 
nor a bail-in of creditors. 
The CBR issued press releases to communicate major decisions with respect to Otkritie’s 
resolution. The content of the press releases did not provide much detai l on the specifics of 
the resolution, with more detailed information being provided via media interviews and 
similar press correspondence. For instance, when announcing the bailout of Otkritie, the CBR 
published a press release simply stating that it would provide “financial support to [Otkritie] 
and guarantee the continuity of operations” through the newly created FBSC without a 
moratorium on payments or a bail -in of creditors, additionally noting the appointment of a 
temporary administration for the bank (CBR 2017d). The same day, CBR Deputy Chair Tulin 
communicated to the press a more detailed explanation of the new resolution mechanism, 
resolution timeline, and recapitalization plan, among other relevant conditions (Dementyeva 
et al. 2017). 
The CBR’s initial announcement of the Otkritie intervention was neutral in tone and did not 
indicate the cause of the bank’s distress (Lokshina, Gaidev, and Polyakova 2017) . Weeks 
later, on September 14, CBR Governor Nabiullina addressed the situation in more detail, this 
time stating that Otkritie had engaged in excessive risk  taking and implying that Otkritie’s 
purchase of 2030 Eurobonds (dollar -denominated Russian sovereign debt), of which 
Otkritie owned the majority issuance, enabled the bank to manipulate the bond’ s market 
price and mask the bank’s capital hole (Nabiullina 2017). Nabiullina also stated that the CBR

planned to legally challenge the payment of executive bonuses for the first half of 2017, just

The initial news of the CBR’s intervention was received positively by the market with shares

7. Source and Size of Funding:  The CBR funded the resolution through the creation 
of reserves. 
The CBR funded Otkritie’s resolution including recapitalizations and loans (deposits) 
through allocations from the CBR’s FBSC (ultimately, through the CBR’s creation of reserves) 
(Mokeeva and Popova 2020) . The CBR injected capital of RUB 498.9 billion through the 
purchase of newly issued ordinary shares in two separate rounds (CBR 2019). In December 
2017, the CBR injected RUB 456.2 billion: RUB 189.1 billion to cover the capital deficit, RUB 
182 billion for new capital to the bank, and the remainder  was allocated for financial 
assistance to select subsidiaries of Otkritie’s parent company (Rosgosstrakh insurance 
company and nonstate pension funds) (CBR 2017h). In June 2018, the CBR injected a second 
and final round of capital in Otkritie amounting to RUB 42.7 billion for financial support to 
various subsidiaries to compensate for the write-downs on claims between the entities (CBR 
2018b).  
The CBR also extended loans to Otkritie, amounting to RUB 621.5 billion and RUB 174 billion 
outstanding by the end of 2017  and 2018, respectively  (Otkritie 2018b; Otkritie 2019) . Of 
the RUB 621.5 billion, RUB 561.5 billion came in the form of CBR deposits (Otkritie 2019).  
For the creation in 2017 of the FBSC AMC, which managed the resolution assets of Otkritie, 
the CBR contributed RUB 1.5 billion in capital, for full ownership (CBR 2019). 
8. Approach to Resolution  and Restructuring: The CBR first recapitalized Otkritie 
and B&N, which transferred their toxic assets to a bad bank, and subsequently 
merged the two bailed-out banks. 
The CBR’s resolution of Otkritie and subsequent restructuring constituted the first use of the 
CBR’s Fund for Banking Sector Consolidation, the new resolution mechanism established on 
May 2, 2017—coinciding with the passage of amendments to the Bankruptcy Law (Mokeeva 
and Popova 2020) . The FBSC was a newly created subdivision of the CBR . During th e 
resolution process, the CBR would transfer acquired assets ( that is, bank shares) in trust to 
the separate limited liability company —the FBSC Asset Management Company —in which 
the CBR held 100% ownership . Thanks to the legal amendments, the new mechanism  
enabled the CBR to participate directly in the capital of Otkritie and other banks, in contrast 
to Russia’s historic, lending -based approach to bankruptcy, wherein the Deposit Insurance 
Agency—largely funded by the CBR —would extend a cheap, long-term loan to another

investor willing to take over the bank in resolution  (Fitzgeorge-Parker 2017; Karpova and 
Kruglikova 2018).  
Toward the end of August  2017, after  Otkritie received substantial  CBR liquidity support 
(including unsecured), its shareholde rs requested that the CBR intervene using the new 
FBSC (Interfax 2017a). Although at least one other private bank volunteered to participate 
in Otkritie’s bailout  under the old resolution scheme ( that is, by receiving a loan from the 
CBR), the CBR opted for the new scheme by directly recapitalizing the bank (Dementyeva et 
al. 2017; Interfax 2020). The CBR’s motivation for using the FBSC was to avoid the exorbitant 
costs and multiyear resolution process associated with the older lending -based mechanism 
(CBR 2018c).  
On August 30, 2017, the CBR took the first step in the resolution of Otkritie by appointing a 
provisional administration, largely composed of CBR employees (CBR 2018c; Otkritie 2018a; 
WPS USA 2017b) . While the provisional administration audited Ot kritie’s financials to 
determine its capital needs, the CBR guaranteed the continuity of Otkritie’s operations by 
extending loans and placing deposits at Otkritie using the FBSC (CBR 2018c). In the process 
of the audit, Otkritie revealed negative capital of RUB 188.9 billion on October 1, 2017, which 
grew to RUB 300 billion as of November 1, 2017 (Interfax 2017b; Seddon 2017f; WPS 
2017b). Owing to the capital shortfall, by the end of September 2017, the CBR announced its 
intention to bail-in Otkritie’s subordinated debt. as a prerequisite to recapitalizing the bank 
(Fojcik 2017a; Interfax 2017b). On November 29, 2017, following the passage of additional 
bankruptcy amendments, which expanded the powers of the provisional administration of a 
bank in resolution to enable bail -in, the  CBR transferred the powers of the provisional 
administration to the FBSC AMC. On November 30, 2017, the new provisional administration 
wrote down authorized capital to RUB 1, and in December, the CBR recapitalized Otkritie by 
purchasing newly issued shares for RUB 456.2 billion (99.9% of the bank’s equity) (Otkritie 
2018a). Of the RUB 456.2 billion, RUB 189.1 billion covered the capital deficit, RUB 182 
billion provided  new capital to the bank, and the remainder was allocated for financial 
assistance to select subsidiaries of Otkritie’s parent company (Rosgosstrakh insurance 
company and nonstate pension funds) (CBR 2017h).  
The recapitalization aimed for Otkritie to meet regulatory capital requirements , and in 
December 2017, the work of the temporary administration ceased with the appointment of 
new permanent management (CBR 2017h; Otkritie 2018a). By January 1, 2018, Otkritie did 
not meet  regulatory capital requirements, and on January 1 9, 2018, the CBR decided to 
continue implementing resolution measures (Otkritie 2018a).  
The CBR injected a further RUB 42.7 billion into Otkritie in June 2018, to be used to extend 
financial sup port to various subsidiaries to compensate for the write -downs on claims 
between the entities (CBR 2018b).  
Following Otkritie’s bailout, the CBR also intervened in bailing out several other banks: B&N 
in September 2017 , Promsvyazbank in December  2017, Asia-Pacific Bank in April 2018 , 
Moscow Industrial Bank in January 2019, and Vokbank in April 2019 (Interfax 2019). These

In July 2019, the CBR completed its restructuring and resolution measures for Otkritie, with 
the bank having met its rehabilitation targets . In 2019, Otkritie paid div idends of RUB 2 
billion for 2018. Thereafter, the CBR developed a plan to divest its stake in Otkritie (CBR

9. Treatment of Creditors and Equity Holders: The CBR bailed  in almost all of 
Otkritie’s subordinated creditors and wrote down equity to RUB 1. 
In the initial announcement on August 29, the CBR said that creditors would not be bailed in 
as part of the rehabilitation of Otkritie Bank (CBR 2017d).  Under Russia’s Bankruptcy Law, 
a recapitalization from the CBR require d it to take over at least 75% of the bank’s share 
capital (Mokeeva and Popova 2020) . Toward the end of September, the CBR’s temporary 
administration discovered a capital hole in Otkritie’s balance sheet (amounting to RUB 188.9 
billion as of October 1, 2017), and the CBR moved to bail in subordinated creditors and write 
down equity to RUB 1, a prerequisite for the recapitalization (Astapenko 2017; Interfax 
2017b; WPS USA 2017b). After the passage of a new amendment to the Bankruptcy Law in 
November 2017, which expanded the temporary administration’s bail -in powers, the 
temporary administration promptly bailed  in RUB 160 billion of subordinated debt and 
wrote down share capital  to RUB 1 (Fojcik 2017b). Following the CBR’s re capitalization in 
December 2017, the CBR owned 99.9% of Otkritie’s ordinary shares (CBR 2018c). 
Before bailing in subordinated liabilities, Otkritie, under the supervision of the FBSC, repaid 
some subordinated loans of RUB 6.9 billion (due in 2019) to state-owned Vnesheconombank, 
the proceeds of which were transferred to the Russian National Wealth Fund (Fojcik 2017b).

10. Treatment of Clients: The CBR opted not to impose a moratorium on payments to 
depositors and other clients, ensuring the continuity of Otkritie’s operations. 
When announcing the bailout of Otkritie, the CBR noted that it would not impose a 
moratorium on payments to depositors and other clients (CBR 2017d). Historically in Russia, 
a moratorium on payments accompanies the appointment of a tempor ary administration 
(the beginning of the bank’s resolution process)  (Dementyeva et al. 2017) . Following the 
initial resolution announcement, CBR Deputy Chair Tulin said that the CBR did not expect  
the bailout to cause contagion, noting that if the CBR had imposed a moratorium on satisfying 
the bank’s obligations, there would be such risks (Dementyeva et al. 2017). 
11. Treatment of Assets: Otkritie transferred toxic and noncore assets to a bad asset 
bank by spinning off a bridge bank, which subsequently merged with a new toxic-
asset bank. 
In May 2018, the CBR communicated a plan  to transfer RUB 2 trillion of bad assets from 
banks taken over by the FBSC into Trust NAB —a new noncore asset bank , or bad bank , 
created from Otkritie’s former subsidiary, Trust Bank (Raisky and Dementieva 2018) . The 
RUB 2 trillion bad assets comprised RUB 539 billion from Otkritie, RUB 332 billion from 
Trust, RUB 139 billion from B&N, RUB 745 billion from Rost, and RUB 315 billio n from 
Promsvyazbank and AVB bank (Raisky and Dementieva 2018) . The creation of Trust NAB 
was completed in July 2018, and the CBR’s FBSC held a 97% stake in the bad bank with the 
remainder being owned by Otkritie (2%) and the Russian Direct Investment Fund  (1%) 
(Fojcik 2018a). Two of Otkritie’s board members were each selected to lead the process of 
establishing Trust NAB and its ongoing operations , respectively (Fojcik 2018a; Raisky and 
Dementieva 2018).  
The CBR decided that Otkritie would restructure by spinning  off its bad assets, through the 
creation of a bridge bank, Bank Otkritie Special JSC, which would hold a banking license for 
one day before merging with Trust NAB. 5 The reason for this unconventional restructur ing 
mechanism was  to bypass required tax payments in the transfer of assets (Raisky and 
Dementieva 2018) . Under the Russian tax code, absent a court decision, nonperforming 
loans cannot be transferred for free or for a symbolic amount , and loans related to  former 
owners cannot be discounted (Raisky and Dementieva 2018).

approval of the CBR’s plan to participate in bankruptcy prevention of the bank . Such 
obligations do not include payroll co ntracts (excluding bonuses and other incentive 
compensation) (Russian Federation 2002d, art. 189.49).

resolution (after satisfying the bankruptcy prevention conditions) until receiving 
permission from the CBR, the bank is prohibited from making decisions related to the 
distribution of profits to its founders,  payment or announcement of dividends, and share 
repurchases, if these decision s lead to noncompliance in the CBR’s mandatory ratios  
(Russian Federation 2002d, art. 189.11) . If the persons controlling the bank are found 
responsible for its bankruptcy, they are held  responsible for subsidiary liabilities in the 
amount equal to the unpaid creditors (Russian Federation 2002d, arts. 61 and 189.23).

15. Duration: The CBR completed the resolution of Otkritie in July 2019 and sold the 
bank to VTB in December 2022 for RUB 340 billion. 
The CBR’s initial plan did not specify an exit date but estimated the rehabilitation would take 
six to eight months divided into two stages . In the first stage , the CBR would appoint a 
provisional administration, assess Otkritie’s financials, and determine the level of necessary 
capitalization and bail-in measures. In the second stage, the CBR would recapitalize the bank 
and appoint new management (Dementyeva et al. 2017; WPS USA 2017b). The CBR intended 
to eventually sell Otkritie back to the market, with CBR Governor Nabiullina stating, “We do 
not seek to increase the state’s share in the banking sector” (Nabiullina 2017).  
Toward the end of September 2017, the CBR said that it planned to sell Otkritie (and B&N 
Bank) in two years, depending on market conditions (Prime News 2017a). In 2018, the CBR 
decided to restructure Otkritie and merged it with B&N Bank in January 2019 (Fojcik 2019). 
By December 2018, Otkritie had returned all its borrowed CBR funds (WPS USA 2018). The 
CBR completed the resol ution of Otkritie in July 2019 (CBR 2020). In December 2019, the 
CBR stated that it aimed to sell Otkritie in 2021 , beginning with an initial public offering 
(IPO) of 20–25% of its shares (Interfax 2019).  
However, owing to market conditions at that time, the CBR could not sell Otkritie at IPO and 
opted to sell the bank to state -owned VTB Bank. In December 2022, the CBR sold 100% of 
its stake in Otkritie to VTB Bank for RUB 340 billion. The passage of a new law in March 2022 
enabled the CBR to sell shares in Otkritie at the market value, as estimated by an independent 
appraiser at RUB 328 billion to RUB 374 billion. VTB paid RUB 233.1 billion in cash and RUB 
106.9 billion in federal government bonds (CBR 2022).
