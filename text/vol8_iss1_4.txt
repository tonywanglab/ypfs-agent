Lessons Learned 
 
Eugen Rădulescu  
By Benjamin Hoffner 
Eugen Rădulescu has worked at the National Bank of Romania (NBR) since 1990 in various 
roles covering monetary policy, foreign exchange, and supervision. During his tenure , he 
contributed to the NBR’s work to fortify Romanian’s post -communist banking laws and the 
operational structure of its financial system to bring it up to the European standard, enabling 
Romania to join the European Union (EU) in 2007. Following the Global Financial Crisis  of 
2007–09 (GFC), Rădulescu was named director of the NBR’s Financial Stability Department and 
has served in this role until June 2023,when he became senior adviser to the governor of the 
NBR. This Lessons Learned summary is based on an interview conducted by the Yale Program 
on Financial Stability in December 2022; the full transcript may be found here. 
Acting swiftly and preemptively at the early signs of a crisis can help minimize direct 
and indirect impacts as the crisis develops. 
Rădulescu reflected on Romania’s experience during the GFC and its aftermath, drawing on 
over three decades of institutional evolution at the NBR. A key lesson Rădulescu emphasized 
was the need for timely and proactive crisis engagement. Romania did not fa ce a classic 
banking panic or the collapse of toxic asset holdings during the height of the crisis, but it was 
hit hard by second -order effects of the GFC. Demand from Western Europe collapsed, 
domestic wages fell, unemployment rose, and real sector vulner abilities quickly translated 
into financial distress.  
In response, Romanian authorities moved rapidly to secure external support and coordinate 
a credible stabilization framework. Rădulescu described the Vienna Initiative —an 
unprecedented public -private c risis coordination effort across Central and Eastern 
Europe—as essential to forestalling a deeper financial collapse. Under the initiative, 
Romania negotiated with the nine largest foreign -owned banks operating in the country . 
“What we did was have an agreement with the first nine banks in Romania . . . that they will 
not withdraw their capital lower than the level in March 2009,” he explained.  
The agreement included the International Monetary Fund  (IMF), European Commission 
(EC), European Central Bank (EC B), and private institutions , and created space for market 
stabilization. It was backed by a EUR 20 billion support package with contributions from the 
IMF, World Bank, and European Investment Bank. This package, Rădulescu emphasized, did 
not involve bank bailouts but bolstered confidence by signaling Romania’s commitment to a 
credible policy program. 
The international reserves, which were held at the central bank as part of the agreement, 
had a calming effect on markets, said Rădulescu.  
It was important b ecause it bought confidence. Once we had this amount in our 
reserves, and also the seal of approval that our policies were the right ones, the

financial market calmed down, and it was possible for us to finance the needs for the 
deficits, both the government deficit and the current account deficit.  
He framed the move as preemptive, underscoring that the agreement was made before full -
scale panic took hold in domestic financial markets. 
Rădulescu contrasted this approach to more reactive strategies taken in  other countries, 
noting that securing early international cooperation and implementing credible domestic 
measures were key to Romania’s relative stability. “We were able to act preemptively . . . not 
in the sense that we acted before September 15, 2008, and Lehman Brothers, but before the 
crisis reached our system full force.” 
The Vienna Initiative also reflected lessons from previous crises, including the transition 
shocks that had exposed Romania’s financial fragility in the 1990s. Having experienced bouts 
of triple-digit inflation and structural reforms with minimal investor confidence, Romanian 
authorities were acutely aware of the costs of delay. By 2009, this historical memory shaped 
a consensus to act early and avoid a drawn-out liquidity crunch or capital flight. 
The strategic coordination of the Vienna Initiative helped Romania avoid the fate of other 
emerging markets that saw large -scale capital withdrawals or sovereign distress. While 
nonperforming loans rose sharply, systemic collapse was avoided, and bank recapitalization 
proceeded without direct government injections. For Rădulescu, the lesson was clear: 
preemptive engagement —especially with external partners  when needed —can prevent a 
crisis from spiraling.  
Maintaining a well-capitalized banking sector and appropriate provisioning for loans 
can buffer the initial effects of a crisis and also will enable a more resilient recovery. 
A second key lesson drawn by Rădulescu from Romania’s GFC experience is the critical role 
of strong bank capitalization—both as a buffer in the face of crisis and as a foundation for 
credible, market-driven recovery. Although Romania avoided direct exposure to subprime 
assets and complex securitizations, its banks were nonetheless vulnerable owing to a rapid 
expansion of credit in the precrisis years fueled by foreign capital inflows and growing 
household indebtedness. 
Between 2000 and 2007, Romanian banks increased lending at a pace far outstripping 
domestic deposit growth. The loan -to-deposit ratio clim bed from approximately 70% to 
120%, with most of the excess funded through external borrowing from parent institutions 
in Western Europe. As real sector conditions deteriorated in 2009, this dynamic left 
Romanian banks exposed to rising defaults, and by 2011, the proportion of nonperforming 
loans had reached a staggering 23%. 
Despite the scale of the NPL problem, Romania stood out in the region for avoiding public 
recapitalization of its banks. Instead, as Rădulescu explained, the central bank required 
banks and their parent companies to take responsibility for recognizing losses and 
rebuilding capital buffers through internal measures. This process began with a thorough 
reassessment of collateral values —many of which had fallen sharply, particularly in re al

estate. Some land values outside city centers had declined by as much as 80%.  Rădulescu 
elaborated on the steps in the process: 
First, it was to evaluate what is the actual value of their collateral and then to 
provision everything which was not provisi oned. And in a few years, the banks 
started to increase their capital in order to keep their capital requirements in line 
with our regulation. We were one of the five countries in the whole European Union 
where an intervention of the government was not nec essary. So, there was no 
financing of the financial system with the intervention of the government. 
The NBR supervised these efforts closely, relying on auditors from major international firms 
to verify data and ensure consistency. 
To enforce discipline an d ensure progress, the central bank  also introduced a novel 
macroprudential rule —a systemic risk buffer targeted specifically at the NPL problem. 
Banks were required to keep nonperforming loans below 5% of total loans and provisioning 
rates above 60%. Inst itutions failing to meet either target faced an additional 1% capital 
buffer requirement. This policy created strong incentives for banks to clean up their balance 
sheets and align with prudential norms without resorting to public funds , Rădulescu 
explained. This approach was seen as fair and efficient, rewarding progress while imposing 
costs on laggards. Over time, the policy contributed to a steady decline in NPLs and restored 
market confidence in the banking system’s solvency. 
Rădulescu emphasized that R omania’s ability to resolve a severe banking sector shock 
without fiscal transfers was a point of national credibility. While some critics noted that 
banks paid no profit taxes for seven years during the recapitalization period, the central bank 
viewed this as an acceptable trade -off for long -term stability: “ Well, yes, but t hey covered 
their own losses without an intervention of the government.” 
This hard-earned resilience became evident during the COVID -19 crisis. Romanian banks, 
with stronger capital buf fers and more conservative lending profiles, weathered the 
downturn without a comparable surge in defaults. In Rădulescu’s view, the investments in 
recapitalization and provisioning during the GFC had positioned the financial system to 
function as a stabilizer rather than a transmission channel for risk. 
Effective cris is prevention requires regulatory innovation, the recognition of  
emerging risks, and the persistence to defend sound policy despite skepticism. 
The third major lesson Rădulescu identified was the importance of both regulatory 
innovation and designing solutions that fit the domestic economy. He illustrated his point by 
describing the central bank’s early integration of macroprudential stability with 
microprudential controls tailored to borrower behavior. While macro -level capital buffers 
and supervision are necessary, said Rădulescu, Romania’s experience demonstrated that 
more was needed , as unchecked household borrowing and foreign currency exposure had 
created significant vulnerabilities in the country’s rapidly developing banking sector.

As early as 2004, Radulescu said, the National Bank of Romania recognized these risks and 
began implementing a set of borrower -focused prudential measures —particularly debt-
service-to-income (DSTI) ratios and loan-to-value (LTV) limits. These tools were intended to 
rein in  high levels of household indebtedness and curb speculative credit growth as 
Romania’s financial system expanded amid strong capital inflows and rising wages . At the 
time, some Romanian households were taking on monthly loan payments that exceeded their 
entire income, exposing the system to significant default risk. 
The central bank’s early adoption of these tools proved timely. Between 2004 and 2007, the 
financial system grew by 50  to 60% annually, driven in part by liberal credit conditions. 
Concerned that such rapid expansion could lead to a domestic crisis —even independent of 
global developments—Rădulescu and his team moved to cool credit markets through these 
borrower-level constraints. 
However, upon Romania’s accession to the European Union in 2007, these prudential 
measures encountered institutional resistance. EU officials classified DSTI and LTV 
requirements as administrative controls, relayed Rădulescu.  
And as long as they were administrative, they were labeled as germinating risks for 
capital inflows, so we had to remove them and leave the market to regulate that. And 
you know that the market, particularly a young market, does not do very well in those 
things.  
Under EU pressure, Romania was required to roll back these regulatory tools, removing a 
key line of defense against excessive household leverage. Rădulescu expressed frustration at 
this reversal, noting that the young Romanian market lacked the maturity to self -regulate 
effectively. As credit growth continued unchecked and foreign -currency loans became 
increasingly common, the seeds were planted for future stress once external conditions 
worsened. 
The GFC vindicated Romania’s earlier concerns. Within a few years, the EU reconsidered its 
stance, reclassifying DSTI and LTV measures as permitted microprudential tools rather than 
administrative controls. These instruments were subsequently adopted across Europe and 
are now part of the standard macroprudential toolkit. 
Romania reintroduced these tools in a simplified and formalized structure. By 2019, DSTI 
thresholds were set at 40% for both domestic-currency loans and foreign -currency loans 
where the borrower had a natural hedge (that is, income in the same currency). For foreign-
currency loans without a natural hedge, the DSTI was limited to 20%.  
The reintroduction of DSTI and LTV limits reflects a broader lesson about institutional 
patience and the value of early action , reflected Rădulescu. Though temporarily discarded 
under external pressure, their eventual restoration confirmed the utility of micro -level 
borrower constraints —especially in environments with high credit growth and limited 
financial literacy.  These measures helped prevent another wave  of risky household 
borrowing ahead of the COVID-19 crisis.

“People usually used to have too rosy expectations,” Rădulescu explained. The reimposed 
borrower-based limits helped focus borrowers and banks on more conservative lending 
practices. He continued:  
And we entered the crisis in 2019 with this limitation in place. Therefore, even if we 
had several troubles associated with the COVID crisis, they were incomparable with 
the troubles that occurred in 2008 –09 to the extent that the trend of declining 
nonperforming loans in the banking sector continued throughout this period. 
Eventually Romania’s level of NPLs fell below 3%—well within green-zone thresholds under 
the European Systemic Risk Board (ESRB) framework. 
This evolution, from early adoption to force d rollback and eventual European 
standardization, illustrates the benefit of regulatory flexibility and long -term commitment 
to borrower -based safeguards. For Rădulescu, the lesson is not just about specific 
instruments, but about the ability to recognize emerging risks and the persistence to 
implement sound but innovative policy even in the face of  skepticism. 
There is great value in taking a long -term view to build financial system stability and 
resilience suited to the domestic environment, especially for emerging markets, even 
in the face of short-term setbacks and skepticism. 
Romania’s financial system has undergone a dramatic transformation since the early 1990s. 
From the outset of post -communist transition, the country faced significant structural 
challenges—a shallow financial sector, inflationary pressures, and minimal inst itutional 
capacity. Yet by the time of the GFC, Romania had established a credible, independent central 
bank, a two -tier banking system, and a growing economy with increasing international 
integration. This institutional groundwork allowed Romania to respond to the crisis not with 
panic, but with a set of measured, targeted interventions shaped by experience and foresight. 
What stands out in Rădulescu’s retelling is the consistency of Romania’s crisis response with 
its long-run strategy for financial stability. Rather than rely on emergency bailouts or ad hoc 
legislative fixes, Romania used the GFC as an opportunity to validate and reinforce its 
prudential framework. The Vienna Initiative demonstrated how international cooperation 
could be used to support do mestic stability without imposing unsustainable conditionality 
or undermining local control.  
By 2022, Romania had one of the lowest NPL ratios in Europe and maintained international 
reserves above EUR 40 billion, underscoring the durability of its financi al sector reforms. 
The COVID -19 pandemic and Russia’s invasion of Ukraine created new macroeconomic 
shocks, including elevated inflation and widening deficits. But unlike in 2008, the banking 
system was not part of the problem —instead, as Rădulescu emphasi zed, it became part of 
the solution. 
Romania’s experience offers a clear set of takeaways for other emerging markets . The 
highlighted principles, which formed the basis of Romania’s crisis -era responses, helped 
strengthen the resilience of the country’s financial system and better prepare it to withstand
