Abstract 
Fortis Group, a financial conglomerate with subsidiaries in Belgium, the Netherlands, and 
Luxembourg, faced share price deterioration and a depositor run a s the Global Financial 
Crisis (GFC) intensified in September 2008. On September 2 9, the three governments 
announced a EUR 11.2 billion bailout package that would allow the Fortis Group to continue 
operating with recapitalized subsidiaries. As part of that deal, Belgium contributed a EUR 4.7 
billion capital injection to the Belgian subsidiary, Fortis Bank Belgium (FBB), which held both 
the group’s Belgian bank and its main Dutch and Luxembourgish banking activities . 
However, the cross-border cooperation fractured after the failure of the recapitalization to 
stem runs by depositors and other creditors. On October 3, Dutch authorities announced that 
the Dutch government  would purchase and temporarily nationalize all of F ortis’s Dutch 
assets for EUR 16.8 billion. That announcement split the group and led the Belgian and 
Luxembourg governments to also change strategic course, focusing on rescuing Fortis’s 
subsidiaries while also separating them from the group . On October 5, Belgium announced 
it would purchase the remaining 50% of FBB from Fortis Group for EUR 4.7 billion, bringing 
its investment to EUR 9.4 billion for a 99.9% stake, and that it would then sell 75% of the 
bank to BNP Paribas, a large French bank, in exchange for new BNP Paribas ordinary shares. 
The plan also created a “bad bank,” structured as a special purpose vehicle (SPV) , called 
Royal Park Investments (RPI) , to assume FBB’s portfolio of troubled structured credit 
securities, largely linked t o US subprime mortgages . The Belgian government had no legal 
authority at the time to impose losses on a failed bank’s equity shareholders or creditors; as 
such, the transactions required the approval of Fortis Group shareholders.  After extended 
negotiations, shareholders approved the plan in April 2009, and the company completed the 
transaction with BNP Paribas on May 12, 2009. Since 2009, the government has sold its 25% 
stake in BNP Paribas for EUR 3.2 billion, its 43.5% stake in the SPV for EUR 1.0 billi on, and 
roughly half its shares in BNP Paribas for EUR 4.1 billion . As of October 2023, it still held 
5.1% of BNP Paribas, valued at about EUR 3.4 billion.

Summary Evaluation 
A report by the Basel Committee noted that, at the time of the Fortis interventi on, Belgium 
lacked the necessary legislation to permit authorities to override the rights of shareholders. 
Negotiations with shareholders resulted in a significant and costly delay. The negotiations 
resulted in the government granting the former shareholders the rights of any upside it 
might have made on the shares it received in BNP Paribas in return for the recapitalization, 
although the government did not realize an upside when it sold those shares  in 2017 and 
2023 (Basel Committee 2010; BNP Paribas 2013; Retail Banker International 2023). 
The Basel Committee also stated that the Fortis restructuring illustrated the tension between 
the domestic focus of national authorities and the cross -border natu re of large financial 
institutions. Despite long -standing relationships and information sharing between Dutch 
and Belgian supervisory authorities, their different assessments of the situation led to 
solutions along national lines that complicated the resolution (Basel Committee 2010).  
A Yale Program on Financial Stability (YPFS) report similarly commented on the cross -
border resolution issues made clear by the Fortis intervention. The report notes that under 
the Single Supervisory Mechanism (SSM), which th e EU established in 2014 to supervise  
banks in the Eurozone and Bulgaria , the original acquisition of ABN AMRO may have been 
significantly delayed, if not completely blocked . This is  because the S ingle Resolution 
Mechanism includes a mandate to consider sy stemic risk issues, a feature that did not exist 
in the national regimes at the time . Fortis would have been designated a systemically 
important bank, placing it under the direct supervision of the European Central Bank (ECB) 
in collaboration with national authorities. The resolution framework under the SSM may also 
have been smoother because the ECB would have funded the original plan, ensuring that it 
was not subject to alteration by individual countries. Additionally, the mechanism could have 
arguably created a more collective forum from the beginning, averting cross-border tension 
(Wiggins, Tente, and Metrick 2019; Wiggins, Wedow, and Metrick 2019).

Fortis bail-out provides an example that an EC member state’s restructuring of a bank can

Key Design Decisions 
1. Purpose: Belgian authorities and the leadership of Fortis developed a 
restructuring plan to protect depositors and creditors while ensuring the 
approval of shareholders. 
In June 2008, Fortis Group faced skepticism from the market about the firm’s ability to 
complete a partial acquisition of ABN AMRO. The purchase price for ABN AMRO was EUR 70 
billion, of which Fortis was responsible for EUR 24 billion.  The growing subprime crisis 
severely impacted Fortis’s ability to mobilize the necessary financing for the deal, 
particularly with growing impairments on its EUR 42 billion structured credit portfolio. The 
deterioration of Fortis’s share price throughout the summer was compounded by a depositor 
run at the onset of the GFC in September 2008. The Fortis banking services were systemically 
important in Belgium (FBB), the Netherlands (FBN), and Luxembourg (FBL), because of the 
large positions in domestic markets , significance to public financing,  and the ir role as 
clearing member s of domestic and foreign stock exchanges  (Basel Committee 2010; EC 
2008a; Reuters 2009a). 
On September 26, 2008, the Fortis board of directors concluded that that it could not raise 
sufficient funds in private markets  to cover a EUR 30 billion liquidity deficit  when markets 
reopened on Monday, September 29, 2008. At the time, Fortis had an estimated core equity 
of EUR 30 billion and Tier 1 equity ratio of 9%.  After crisis talks took place involving 
representatives from the three gover nments and the ECB, authorities announced the 
simultaneous injection of capital into the three national banking units of the Fortis Group in 
the evening on September 29, 2008. The plan stated that each country would acquire a 49%

Fortis 2008a; White 2008). 
However, between September 29 and October 3, 2008, deposit withdrawals continued, and 
Fortis remained frozen out of interbank markets. This continued lack of market confidence 
led Dutch authorities to negotiate with the leadership of Fortis G roup. Dutch authorities 
announced the full nationalization of FBN, the legal body encompassing the Dutch operations 
of Fortis Group, on October 3, 2008. The Dutch purchase totaled EUR 16.8 billion, a price that 
valued ABN AMRO and Fortis Insurance at EUR 9  billion, compared to the EUR 12 billion 
valuation that investors expected. The Dutch action resulted in the uncoupling of FBN from 
FBB and FBL, and Belgian authorities responded by seeking out a large bank to take over the 
Belgian arm of the company (NBB 2009, 30). The Dutch prime minister stated that , “Most 
problems lay in the Belgian part of Fortis. We wanted to make sure the Dutch activities 
wouldn't get into problems ” (Van de Pol 2008) . The only bank that indicated interest was 
BNP Paribas. On October 5, 2008, the Belgian government announced that it would 
temporarily nationalize FBB by purchasing the remaining 50% stake from Fortis Group for 
EUR 4.7 billion . In their announcement on October 5, 2008, Belgian authorities reiterated 
that the new plan represented their commitment to protect depositors and customers (EC 
2008a; Kingdom of Belgium 2008; NBB 2009; Van de Pol 2008).

legal pushback from shareholders in response to losses on the failed bank’s equity. As such, 
the approval of Fortis SA/NV shareholders was necessary to complete the sale  (see Key

restructuring in February 2009. Authorities renegotiated the plan and shareholders granted 
their approval in April 2009  (EC 2008a; European Parliament 2018; Fortis Bank 2009;

2. Part of a Package: The restructuring plan followed an emergency liquidity facility 
and multiple capital injections , and the Belgian state also provided financing 
guarantees for Fortis and the “bad bank” special purpose vehicle. 
On September 29, 2008, the Belgian Cen tral Bank provided emergency liquidity for FBB in 
the form of overnight advances  amounting to around EUR 50 billion . Depositor runs 
continued, and i n October 2008, t he Belgian state bought new shares of FBB for EUR 4.7 
billion, recapitalizing Fortis’s banking subsidiary, and then purchased the remainder of FBB 
from Fortis for an additional EUR 4.7 billion, alongside similar measures by Luxembourg for 
FBL (EC 2008a; Vistesen 2012). These capital injections are discussed in further detail in the 
corresponding ad hoc capital injections case (George, forthcoming).  
To secure the approval of shareholders, the final restructuring plan included several new 
measures. Alongside the state guarantee provided to the commercial paper funding program 
for the SPV (see Key Design Decision No. 7, Source and Size of Funding), Belgian authorities 
also provided a guarantee on the structured credit portfolio retained by FBB (“in” portfolio). 
At year-end 2009, the nominal value of the portfolio was EUR 21 billion, and the state agreed 
to a framework in which FBB bore first losses up to EUR 3.5 billion  and the Belgian state 
would compensate FBB for any losses beyond this point, up to EUR 1.5 billion (EC 2009).   
The state guaranteed a EUR 1 billion loan from FBB to Fortis SA/NV at a cost of 70 basis 
points. The state also guaranteed the payment of interest on the relative performance note  
used to repay convertible and subordinated hybrid equity-linked securities (CASHES), which 
were obligations on the FBB balance sheet.  The prog ram prevented Fortis SA/NV from 
having to pay FBB up to EUR 2.4 billion on the CASHES securities in the short term (EC 2009). 
The Belgian state agreed to purchase up to EUR 2 billion of Tier 1 capital in any form issued 
by FBB, with the goal of ensuring a Tier 1 ratio of 9.2% . Authorities committed to this 
guarantee for a period of three years. The Belgian state also provided a call option such that

3. Legal Authority: The Law of 2 April 1962 established the Federal Participation and 
Investment Company as the agency responsible for state participation in financial 
institutions. 
The Law of 2 April 1962 established the Federal Participation and Investment Company 
(SFPI) in Belgium. Article 3 of the law empowered the agency to hold some or all of the shares 
of a public limited company. However, the law required that SFPI purchase at least 75% of 
the capital when intervening in a financial institution (Belgium 1962). 
The pivotal piece of the restructuring plan, the sale of FBB to BNP Paribas, was significantly 
delayed by legal issues.  An FBB competitor submitted complaints alleging a distortion of 
competition in the Belgian market caused by the state intervention into Fortis. On November 
20, 2008, a group of shareholders filed a complaint on the basis that the Belgia n state sold 
the assets of FBB at too low a price.  On December 12, 2008, the Brussels Court of Appeal 
ruled that the Fortis SA/NV board of directors required approval from Fortis SA/NV 
shareholders for the sale of FBB to BNP Paribas. Specifically, the vote was to be taken among 
shareholders who had held shares prior to October 14, 2008 (Agence France-Presse 2008; 
Blenkinsop 2008; EC 2008a; EC 2009). 
The restructuring of Fortis required final approval from the European Commission (EC) (EC 
2009). 
4. Administration: Belgian authorities collaborated with the leadership of Fortis and 
BNP Paribas to negotiate the restructuring plan. 
The Belgian government injected capital into Fortis through the SFPI. SFPI was an 
independent legal entity created to undertake investme nt transactions that did not meet 
economic criteria but held societal value (SFPI 2009) . As such, SFPI had four major 
mandates: 
• Stimulate the development and establishment of private enterprises 
• Promote economic public initiative 
• Contribute to the execution of the economic strategy of the state 
• Give opinions at the request of the federal government 
5. Governance: SFPI was accountable to the minister of finance and the minister of 
economic affairs. 
The law of April 2,  1962, outlined the governance structure for SFPI. SFPI received its 
assignments from royal decrees or special laws enacted by the Belgian Council of Ministers.

SFPI itself fell under the purview of the minister of finance and the minister of economic 
affairs. These two ministers jointly proposed a commissioner for the king to appoint as a 
liaison between the government and the board of directors responsible for th e daily 
operations of SFPI (Belgium 1962).  
The board of directors consisted of at least 12 members and two independent directors, and 
government officials at all levels were prohibited from being part of it. The Council of 
Ministers appointed the members of the board of directors by royal decree, while the king 
created a jury to propose the independent directors (SFPI 2014 a; SFPI 2014 b; Belgium 
1962).    
6. Communication and Disclosure: Belgian authorities and Fortis leadership aimed 
to project calm by ensuring that all depositors and creditors would be protected , 
but shareholders objected to the resolution plan proposed on October 5, 2008. 
Authorities from Belgium, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg announced a multilateral EUR 
11.2 billion capital injection into Fortis on the evening of September 28, 2008.  Regarding the 
bailout, the Belgian prime minister stated that it showed savers and investors that Fortis 
would not be allowed to fall victim to the GFC. The Dutch finance minister stated that the lack 
of market confidence was unjustified, and that the bank was sound (EC 2008a; White 2008). 
After continued depositor runs, the Dutch government nationalized FBN on October 3, 2008. 
Regarding this unilateral move, the Dutch prime minister stated most of t he group’s 
problems were in  FBB, and that the Dutch move was intended to insulate Fortis’s Dutch 
activities. During intervention talks with Luxembourg ish authorities, the Belgian prime 
minister stated his intention to keep Fortis’s Belgian and Luxembourg ish operations 
together. He emphasized that authorities were in conversation with several private groups, 
and that the goal was to send a strong signal to markets prior to open ing (ENP Newswire 
2008; Macmahon 2008; Van de Pol 2008).  
Belgian authorities issu ed a press release on October 5, 2008 , announcing that the 
government would purchase the remaining shares of Fortis Bank Belgium from Fortis SA/NV 
for EUR 4.7 billion and that it had agreed to sell 75% of the bank to BNP Paribas. The press 
release stated that the additional measures aimed to ensure the “lasting solvability” of Fortis 
Bank Belgium and reiterated the government’s commitment to protecting depositors and 
customers (Kingdom of Belgium 2008).  Fortis SA/NV issued a press release on October 14, 
2008, announcing all of the measures and the new structure of the group in terms of its Dutch 
and Belgian operations (Fortis 2008b; Kingdom of Belgium 2008). 
The Belgian prime minister resigned in December 2008 after being accused of trying to 
influence the court’s decision to delay the restructuring measures (AP 2008).

purchased by BNP Paribas, and public-private sources funded the SPV. 
Before the BNP Paribas restructuring, Belgium’s SFPI injected EUR 4.7 billion of capital into 
FBB in the form of new ordinary shares . In October 2008, it purchased the remaining,

the structured credit portfolio. RPI acquired the portfolio for approximately EUR 11.7 billion, 
resulting in a significant write-down for Fortis SA/NV. The parties funded RPI with EUR 1.7 
billion of e quity and EUR 9.7 billion of debt. The Belgian state took an equity stake of EUR 
740 million, and BNP Paribas increased its previously agreed upon equity stake to EUR 200 
million. The remaining capital was held by Fortis SA/NV, which changed its name to Ageas 
in 2010 (see Key Design Decision  No. 1 5, Duration) (EC 2009; Fortis 2010b; Fortis Bank 
2010; Market News Publishing 2009). 
Alongside the EUR 1.7 billion equity layer, RPI funded itself through issuing two tranches of 
debt, each amounting to EUR 4.8 billion for a total of EUR 9.7 billion. FBB subscribed to the 
super-senior tranche, which consisted of a revolving loan that carried an interest rate of the 
Inter Bank Offered Rate p lus 20 basis points. The senior tranche consisted of a EUR 485 
million loan from BNP Paribas and a EUR 4.4 billion commercial paper program issued by 
FBB to institutional investors with a guarantee from the Belgian state. The Belgian state 
charged RPI a 70-basis-point fee for guaranteeing the commercial paper program and 
provided a bridge loan during the three-to-four-month period in which the program was 
being set up. The state guarantee of the commercial paper program was a major change from 
the October 2 008 agreement, raising the Belgian state’s exposure to RPI’s senior financing 
from 24% to 90% (EC 2008a; Market News Publishing 2009). 
8. Approach to Resolution  and Restructuring: The Belgian government transferred 
the majority of FBB to BNP Paribas and creat ed an SPV to assume the riskiest 
portfolio of assets. 
The original plans developed by Belgium, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg to multilaterally 
recapitalize each of the  national banking subsidiaries of Fortis Group changed when the 
announcement of the EUR  11.2 billion capital injection failed to slow the run on the bank.  
The Dutch minister of finance stated that the continued lack of confidence had “evaporated 
the joint capital injection ” (Van de Pol 2008) . In response, Dutch authorities unilaterally 
nationalized FBN at a cost of EUR 12.8 billion.  Additionally, the Dutch repaid FBN’s debts at 
a cost of EUR 16 billion and acquired Fortis Insurance Nederland for EUR 4 billion. The Dutch 
acquisition notably also included the Dutch state taking over Fortis’s stake in ABN AMRO .5 
The Dutch chose this method of intervention due to the belief that the majority of Fortis’s 
issues were in its Belgian activities (EC 2008a; Van de Pol 2008).

By splitting Fortis Group , the measures taken by Dutch authorities forced Belgium and 
Luxembourg to amend their approach as well.  On October 5, 2008, Belgian authorities 
announced that the state had purchased the remaining existing shares of FBB from Fortis 
SA/NV, effectively natio nalizing the bank.  Following the acquisition of shares, Belgian 
authorities developed a restructuring plan in which it would sell 75% of its stake in FBB to 
BNP Paribas and transfer the worst performing assets to a n SPV called Royal Park 
Investments. Luxem bourg authorities also announced that they would sell 16% of the 
Luxembourg stake in subsidiary FBL to BNP Paribas as part of the restructuring (EC 2008a; 
Macmahon 2008).  
Completion of the sale to BNP Paribas was subject to  several conditions imposed following 
shareholder legal complaints . On November 4, 2008, a Fortis competitor filed a complaint 
stating that the state’s capital injections distorted the market. On November 6, 2008, a 
Belgian court ruled that a vote by the shareholders of Fortis SA/NV was required in order to 
complete the sale to BNP Paribas, resulting in the scheduling of December shareholder 
meetings. A large institutional shareholder  submitted a complaint to a Belgian court on 
November 20, 2008, alleging that the price paid by BNP Paribas—121 million new shares for 
an 11.6% stake, which would have been worth EUR 8.2 billion based on a EUR 68 share price, 
a 17% premium to the then -market price—was significantly below the real value of FBB at 
the time of the transaction.  The Fortis SA/NV board of directors approved the measures in 
the December 1 shareholder meeting, but the Belgian court of appeals suspended the 
implementation on December 12, 2008. The court ruled that the measures required approval 
from Fortis SA/NV’s shareholders, resulting in authorities scheduling a shareholder meeting 
for February 11, 2009 (Agence France-Presse 2008; Dow Jones Newswires 2008; EC 2008a; 
Fortis Bank 2009).  
At the February 11, 2009 , meeting, shareholders voted against the restructuring measures, 
forcing the management of Fortis and BNP Paribas, as well as public authorities, to 
renegotiate a new deal. Authorities reached a new agreement on March 6, 2009; however, a 
Belgian court again intervened on March 31, 2009, ruling that only shareholders who owned 
Fortis shares prior to October 14, 2008, could vote on the restructuring plan. As a result, the 
Fortis SA/NV board of directors chose to postpone the vote until the meetings of April 28 –
29. Shareholders approved the restructuring plan on A pril 28, 2009.  Authorities included 
several measures to secure the approval of shareholders (see Figure 2 in Key Design Decision 
No. 2, Part of a Package) (Blenkinsop 2008; EC 2009; Fortis Bank 2009; Reuters 2009b). 
On May 12, 2009, the EC submitted its ap proval of the new restructuring plan, and on the 
same day BNP Paribas acquired 75% of FBB  from the Belgian state . In return, Belgium 
received an 11.7% stake in BNP Paribas, which was managed by SFPI.  The Belgian state 
created RPI to assume the riskiest structured credits of Fortis Bank. Pursuant to the 
restructuring plan, RPI assumed the structured credit portfolio at a cost of EUR 11.7 billion. 
RPI sold its portfolio of assets to Lone Star Funds on April 26, 2013  for EUR 6.7 billion . 
Following the sale, th e state recognized a capital gain of EUR 260 million on its initial 
investment (EC 2008a; EC 2009; SFPI 2010; SFPI 2014b; Royal Park Investments n.d.) .

9. Treatment of Creditors and Equity Holders: Creditors did not take any losses and 
equity holders received a call option on BNP Paribas shares after extensive 
negotiations. 
Following the breakdown in the September 2008 bailout, Belgium purchased a further 
50.1% of FBB for EUR 4.7 billion in cash, nationalizing the bank. The basis of the shareholder 
dispute that delayed the implementation of the restructuring  (see Key Design Decision No. 
8, Approach to Resolution and Restructuring ) was that the public communication by Fortis 
had misrepresented the state of affairs. Shareholders argued that the sale to BNP Paribas for 
EUR 8.2 billion did not reflect the true value of Fortis at the time. The EC noted that the price 
paid by BNP Paribas was 0.7 times the book value of FBB (and FBL), consistent with the 
purchase prices of other distressed banks at the time (EC 2008a; European Parliament 2018; 
Fortis Bank 2009). 
In the revised restructuring plan approved in April 2009, authorities granted Fortis SA/NV 
a call option on the 121 million BNP Paribas shares that the state received as payment for

10. Treatment of Clients:  Clients remaining in FBB became clients of BNP Paribas 
Fortis. 
After purchasing a 75% stake from the Belgian government, BNP Paribas took on the clients 
of FBB.  The new BNP Paribas Fortis organized itself around four business l ines; Retail & 
Private Banking, Corporate & Public Bank Belgium, Corporate & Investment Banking, and 
Investment Solutions (Fortis Bank 2010). 
After announcing its rebranding in 2010, Ageas leadership continued to use the Fortis name 
in its dealings with clients for whom they deemed it to be beneficial (Fortis 2010b). 
11. Treatment of Assets:  Royal Park Investments took on the risk iest structured 
credits from Fortis. 
The restructuring plan called for the sale of Fortis’s structured credit portfolio to RPI, a “bad 
bank” SPV formed on November 20, 2008. The risky assets in the portfolio included asset-
backed securities, mortgage-backed securities, and collateralized debt obligations, and it had 
a face value of EUR 20.2 billion. RPI officially purchased the port folio on May 12, 2009, for 
EUR 11.7 billion.  The sale lowered the risk-weighted assets  of FBB  by 27%, positively 
impacting the Tier 1 ratio (Fortis Bank 2009; Fortis Bank 2010). 
Part of the structured credit portfolio remained on the balance sheet of BNP P aribas Fortis, 
amounting to EUR 14.6 billion of exposure as of year-end 2009 (Fortis Bank 2010, 170). The 
Belgian state guaranteed the losses in this portfolio up to EUR 1.5 billion (Fortis Bank 2010, 
170). This guarantee would only be activated following the first EUR 3.5 billion of losses, 
which was far higher than the losses expected by authorities (EC 2009; Fortis Bank 2010).

resigned in connection with the restructuring.

injection (Reuters 2009a). Following the capital injections, displeased minority shareholders 
of Fortis  rejected the proposed new chairman, Étienne Davignon, on December 2, 2008

13. Cross-border Cooperation: The catalyst for the restructuring approach was the ring

The nationalization path taken by the Netherlands in lieu of the September 2008 bailout

Belgian and Luxembourgish authorities worked together to find a solution for FBB and FBL. 
Alongside the Belgian transactions, BNP Paribas purchased a 17% stake in FBL from the 
Luxembourg state in exchange for BNP Paribas shares. FBB itself was a 50% shareholder of 
FBL, so BNP Paribas effectively owned 65% of FBL’s share capital. In exchange, Luxembourg 
became a 1% shareholder in BNP Paribas (BNP Paribas 2009; EC 2008a; Macmahon 2008). 
14. Other Conditions: Belgian authorities set limitations on severance compensation 
for outgoing executives. 
Belgian authorities limited the compensation packages that outgoing executives received 
upon their resignation. According to the  provisional measures, departing executives were 
entitled to a maximum of 12 months’ salary, with potential for 18 months’ salary for 
executives who had served for extended periods (defined as a bout 25 years). Belgium also 
required that Fortis set up independent remuneration c ommittees and present reports 
detailing executive compensation at shareholder meetings (Reuters 2008). 
As part of the restructuring deal, the EC required that BNP Paribas divest 100% of its 
Personal Finance business in Belgium. The EC concluded that this condition would eliminate 
concerns of distortions of competition in the markets of Belgium and Luxembourg  (EC 
2008b).
