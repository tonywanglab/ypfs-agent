## Emergency broad-based bank liquidity program: options for supervisor

### Executive summary

Given a run at a globally significant domestic bank and signs of contagion, the priority is to **arrest the acute liquidity panic quickly**, while avoiding the perception that the facility is a bespoke rescue of one institution. The strongest option is to announce a **broad-based term liquidity facility available to all eligible banks**, using existing collateral frameworks where possible, with clear communication that the central bank is prepared to lend in size against sound collateral.

I would present three main options, plus one contingent add-on:

1. **Term Auction Facility-style program** — broad term funding through regular auctions; best for reducing stigma while retaining control over quantities and pricing.
2. **Fixed-rate, full-allotment term lending/repo facility** — lend whatever eligible banks demand against eligible collateral at a preannounced spread; best when confidence needs a decisive “whatever liquidity is needed” signal.
3. **Expanded-collateral term liquidity facility** — supplement standing facilities by accepting broader but risk-managed collateral, including loan portfolios or high-quality illiquid securities; best if the problem is that banks have assets but not enough standing-facility collateral.
4. **Standby/contingent liquidity insurance option** — useful as an add-on or if stress may recur; less suitable as the sole immediate response to an active run.

My recommendation would be a **hybrid of Options 1 and 2**: announce a broad-based facility immediately, begin with **large term auctions**, and include authority to move to **fixed-rate full allotment** if demand or market indicators show intensifying contagion. Pair this with expanded collateral eligibility and strong anti-stigma disclosure protections.

---

# 1. Policy objective

The program should be framed as addressing **systemwide liquidity stress**, not the condition of one named institution.

Suggested objective:

> “To provide term liquidity to the banking system against sound collateral, support the functioning of money and credit markets, and reduce the risk that liquidity pressures at one institution spread to otherwise viable banks.”

This framing matters. Broad-based emergency liquidity programs have historically been most effective when deployed early in the acute phase of a panic, before liquidity problems force asset fire sales or become solvency problems.

---

# 2. Design principles

The program should follow five principles:

### A. Broad eligibility

Eligible participants should include:

- All domestically licensed banks.
- Domestic branches/subsidiaries of foreign banks, if legally and operationally feasible.
- Systemically important deposit-taking institutions outside the formal banking category, if they are subject to prudential supervision and have eligible collateral.

If the legal framework requires lending only to solvent or viable institutions, the test should be **practical and rapid**, not a full stress-test-style solvency review during the panic.

### B. Lend against collateral, not reputation

The facility should emphasize collateral quality, haircuts, and recourse rather than subjective judgments about whether individual banks are “good” or “bad” during the panic.

### C. Reduce stigma

Stigma is often the central design problem. Borrowers may avoid standing facilities if use signals weakness. Anti-stigma features should include:

- Broad participation.
- Auctions or common access terms.
- No contemporaneous disclosure of borrower identities.
- Aggregate reporting only during the acute phase.
- Delayed borrower-level disclosure only if legally required and preferably after markets stabilize.

### D. Use existing operational infrastructure

To move quickly, the program should rely on:

- Existing payment and settlement systems.
- Existing collateral valuation teams.
- Existing legal agreements where possible.
- Existing standing-facility collateral schedules, expanded by temporary annex if needed.

### E. Preannounce flexibility

The announcement should make clear that the central bank can adjust:

- Auction size.
- Frequency.
- Maturities.
- Collateral eligibility.
- Haircuts.
- Pricing method.

This avoids repeated ad hoc announcements that could signal panic.

# 3. Option 1: Term Auction Facility-style program

## Basic structure

The central bank would conduct regular auctions of term central bank credit to eligible banks.

### Key terms

| Feature | Possible design |
|---|---|
| Eligible participants | All eligible banks with access to standing facilities or approved emergency access |
| Instrument | Collateralized loan or repo |
| Maturities | 28 days initially; add 84 days or 3 months if stress persists |
| Auction size | Large preannounced amounts; scalable |
| Pricing | Uniform-price auction with minimum bid rate |
| Collateral | Standing-facility collateral, possibly expanded |
| Individual limit | Example: no bank may receive more than 10–20% of any auction |
| Settlement | Same-day or T+1/T+2; delayed settlement can reduce stigma but may be less useful in a run |
| Disclosure | Aggregate auction results only |

## Advantages

- **Reduces stigma** relative to the discount window because many banks bid together.
- Creates a market-based pricing mechanism.
- Allows the central bank to control the amount of liquidity injected.
- Generates information about liquidity demand.
- Can be scaled up quickly if auctions are oversubscribed.
- Avoids giving the impression that the facility is for one troubled bank.

## Disadvantages

- Auctions may be too slow for banks facing same-day outflows.
- If auction sizes are too small, the facility may disappoint markets.
- If minimum rates are too high, usage may be weak.
- If all bidders are not filled, the facility may not fully reassure markets during severe contagion.

## Best use case

This is best if the banking system is under stress but markets are still functioning enough that banks can wait for auction settlement.

## Suggested version

Launch with:

- Two weekly auctions.
- 1-month and 3-month maturities.
- A large initial allotment.
- Minimum bid rate at policy rate plus a modest spread.
- Power to increase auction size and frequency immediately.

# 4. Option 2: Fixed-rate full-allotment term liquidity facility

## Basic structure

The central bank offers term funding to all eligible banks at a fixed rate against eligible collateral, with no preset aggregate cap or with a very large announced ceiling.

### Key terms

| Feature | Possible design |
|---|---|
| Eligible participants | All eligible banks |
| Instrument | Collateralized loan or repo |
| Maturities | Overnight, 1 week, 1 month, 3 months; possibly 6 months |
| Pricing | Fixed rate, e.g. policy rate plus 25–75 bps |
| Allotment | Full allotment against eligible collateral |
| Collateral | Standing-facility collateral plus emergency expanded collateral |
| Haircuts | Conservative, preannounced, collateral-specific |
| Disclosure | Aggregate only during acute phase |
| Exit | Rate becomes unattractive as markets normalize; later reduce maturities/frequency |

## Advantages

- Sends the strongest possible signal that liquidity is available.
- Reduces rollover risk.
- Can stop self-fulfilling runs if creditors believe banks can meet withdrawals.
- Simple to communicate.
- Especially useful after a major shock or if auctions are repeatedly oversubscribed.

## Disadvantages

- Greater moral hazard risk.
- Larger central bank balance sheet exposure.
- Higher risk of supporting weak banks for too long.
- Requires strong collateral valuation and risk controls.
- May complicate monetary policy implementation unless sterilized or integrated into reserve management.

## Best use case

This is best if contagion is accelerating and confidence needs a decisive backstop.

## Suggested version

Announce as a temporary emergency facility:

> “The central bank will provide term funding in full against eligible collateral to all eligible banks at a fixed rate, for as long as needed to ensure the smooth functioning of the banking system.”

To preserve discipline, price at a modest premium to normal central bank funding but below distressed market rates.

# 5. Option 3: Expanded-collateral term lending facility

## Basic structure

The central bank supplements standing facilities by allowing banks to borrow against collateral that is sound but not normally eligible, or not normally eligible at term.

This could be structured as:

- A repo facility.
- A collateralized loan facility.
- A securities-lending or collateral-swap facility.
- A term loan facility secured by portfolios of bank loans.

### Possible collateral categories

| Collateral tier | Examples | Haircut approach |
|---|---|---|
| Level A | Domestic sovereign debt, central bank bills | Low haircut |
| Level B | Agency debt, covered bonds, high-quality RMBS, government-guaranteed bank debt | Moderate haircut |
| Level C | High-quality ABS, corporate bonds, commercial paper | Higher haircut |
| Level D | Performing loan portfolios, mortgages, SME loans, own-name securitizations | Conservative haircut, eligibility screens, concentration limits |

## Advantages

- Addresses the common crisis problem: banks may have assets, but not enough assets eligible at standing facilities.
- Reduces fire-sale pressure.
- Supports banks that are liquid on a going-concern basis but temporarily unable to monetize assets.
- Can be paired with either auctions or full-allotment lending.

## Disadvantages

- Valuation risk is higher.
- Operationally more complex.
- Requires strong legal control over collateral.
- May be criticized as accepting “bad assets” unless eligibility and haircuts are transparent.
- Own-name and illiquid collateral require special controls.

## Best use case

This is best if the run is causing banks to exhaust standard collateral or if specific asset classes have become illiquid.

## Suggested version

Use a tiered collateral framework:

- Start with existing standing-facility collateral.
- Add high-quality liquid private securities.
- Add performing loan portfolios only with conservative haircuts and representations.
- Apply extra haircuts for foreign-currency collateral, own-name securities, or less-liquid assets.
- Impose concentration limits by issuer, asset class, and borrower.

# 6. Option 4: Standby liquidity insurance or option facility

## Basic structure

The central bank auctions or grants options that allow banks to draw liquidity later at preannounced terms.

## Advantages

- Can have a positive announcement effect.
- Provides reassurance against future funding spikes.
- Encourages banks to plan liquidity needs in advance.
- May reduce stigma because many firms purchase insurance before they need it.

## Disadvantages

- Less useful when an active bank run is already underway.
- More complex to explain.
- Requires time to operationalize.
- May not reassure depositors unless paired with an immediately available facility.

## Best use case

As a complement once immediate run pressure is contained, or to reassure markets around known future stress dates.

# 7. Recommended package

I would recommend the following package:

## A. Announce a Broad-Based Term Liquidity Facility immediately

Use a neutral name, e.g.:

**Systemwide Term Liquidity Facility**  
or  
**Banking System Liquidity Insurance Facility**

Avoid naming the troubled institution.

## B. Launch with auctions, but preauthorize full allotment

Initial design:

- Weekly or twice-weekly 1-month and 3-month operations.
- Eligible to all domestic banks.
- Collateral: standing-facility collateral plus temporary expanded collateral.
- Uniform-price auctions.
- Large allotments.
- Individual award limits.
- Aggregate auction results published; no borrower names.

Contingency language:

> “If conditions warrant, the central bank may conduct fixed-rate full-allotment operations.”

This gives policymakers the ability to escalate quickly.

## C. Keep a same-day backstop available

Because auctions may not meet urgent outflows, the central bank should maintain or enhance the standing facility for same-day liquidity:

- Reduce the penalty spread temporarily.
- Extend maturity beyond overnight.
- Expand eligible collateral.
- Clarify that use is expected and not stigmatizing.

## D. Expand collateral carefully

Create temporary collateral categories with haircuts:

- Government and agency securities.
- Covered bonds.
- High-quality securitizations.
- Performing loan portfolios.
- High-quality foreign-currency securities if relevant.

Use conservative valuation and daily margining where feasible.

## E. Protect confidentiality

During the acute phase:

- Publish aggregate utilization only.
- Publish auction statistics without names.
- Avoid borrower-level disclosure unless legally unavoidable.
- If disclosure is required, delay it until after a specified period.

## F. Coordinate with fiscal authority if needed

If the collateral expansion or scale creates material credit risk, consider:

- Treasury indemnity.
- Government guarantee for specific collateral classes.
- Temporary statutory authority if existing authority is insufficient.

# 8. Specific term sheet for preferred option

## Systemwide Term Liquidity Facility — draft terms

| Term | Proposed design |
|---|---|
| Purpose | Provide term liquidity to banks to address systemwide funding stress and reduce contagion risk |
| Eligible borrowers | All regulated banks in good standing with the central bank; foreign bank branches if eligible under law |
| Form | Collateralized loan or repo |
| Maturities | 28 days and 84 days initially; option to add 6 months |
| Rate | Auction-determined, subject to minimum of policy rate plus modest spread |
| Escalation | Central bank may switch to fixed-rate full allotment at policy rate plus fixed spread |
| Collateral | Standing-facility collateral plus temporary expanded collateral schedule |
| Haircuts | Based on existing schedule; add premiums for illiquid, foreign-currency, or own-name collateral |
| Frequency | Weekly initially; increase to twice weekly or daily if needed |
| Aggregate size | Large initial allotment; authority to expand without further policy vote |
| Individual cap | 10–20% of each auction, unless full-allotment mode is activated |
| Settlement | T+1 for auctions; same-day emergency bridge via standing facility |
| Disclosure | Aggregate program usage and auction results only; no contemporaneous borrower-level disclosure |
| Exit | Reduce auction size/frequency as conditions normalize; maintain penalty spread as natural exit mechanism |
| Governance | Approved by central bank board/policy committee; daily monitoring by crisis liquidity committee |
| Risk controls | Collateral eligibility review, haircuts, margin calls, concentration limits, supervisory information sharing |

# 9. Communication strategy

The announcement should be short, confident, and systemwide.

Suggested language:

> “The central bank is establishing a temporary Systemwide Term Liquidity Facility to ensure that sound banks can obtain term liquidity against eligible collateral during current market stress. The facility is available to all eligible banks on common terms. It is designed to support the functioning of money and credit markets and to prevent liquidity pressures from impairing the supply of credit to households and businesses.”

Important points to include:

- Facility is broad-based.
- It is temporary.
- It is collateralized.
- It is not a capital support program.
- It supplements existing facilities.
- The central bank is prepared to adjust size and terms as needed.
- Borrower identities will not be disclosed during the acute phase except as required by law.

Avoid:

- Mentioning the troubled bank by name.
- Saying the facility is for “weak” or “distressed” banks.
- Overemphasizing penalty rates.
- Announcing before operational readiness.

# 10. Interaction with other crisis tools

Emergency lending may not be enough if the public doubts bank solvency or deposit safety. If retail or uninsured deposit runs are accelerating, the central bank should brief fiscal authorities and supervisors on possible complementary measures:

- Temporary expansion of deposit insurance.
- Account guarantees for transaction accounts.
- Government guarantees of selected bank liabilities.
- Resolution or recapitalization planning for the globally significant bank if it is not viable.
- Public supervisory statement on the resilience of the banking system.
- Cross-border central bank coordination if the bank is globally active.
- Foreign-currency swap lines if funding pressure is in foreign currency.

Liquidity support can arrest a panic, but it cannot solve a solvency problem. If the troubled global bank is insolvent or likely to fail, the broad-based facility should be paired with a separate resolution or capital strategy.

---

# 11. Key risks and mitigants

| Risk | Mitigant |
|---|---|
| Stigma prevents usage | Use auctions, broad eligibility, aggregate disclosure only |
| Program seen as bailout of one bank | Make terms broad, common, and transparent |
| Moral hazard | Charge modest premium, require collateral, apply haircuts, impose supervisory follow-up |
| Credit losses | Conservative collateral valuation, margining, fiscal indemnity if needed |
| Facility too small | Preauthorize expansion and full-allotment mode |
| Facility too slow | Keep same-day standing facility backstop |
| Legal challenge | Use existing authorities if possible; obtain emergency authorization if needed |
| Monetary policy complications | Sterilize if necessary or incorporate into reserves framework |
| Exit problems | Use pricing, declining auction sizes, shorter maturities, and sunset review |

---

# Bottom line recommendation

Recommend a **broad-based Systemwide Term Liquidity Facility** with:

1. **Immediate announcement and operational readiness.**
2. **Broad eligibility for all banks.**
3. **Term funding through large auctions.**
4. **Authority to shift to fixed-rate full allotment.**
5. **Expanded collateral with conservative haircuts.**
6. **Strong confidentiality protections.**
7. **Clear exit strategy.**
8. **Coordination with fiscal and supervisory authorities in case guarantees, resolution, or recapitalization become necessary.**

This structure balances speed, credibility, stigma reduction, and risk control while ensuring the program is clearly a systemwide liquidity backstop rather than a single-bank rescue.
